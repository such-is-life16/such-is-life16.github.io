Research Overview

under construction

SAX J1712.6–3739 is the only known supersonic low-mass X-ray binary (LMXB) with a resolved bow shock nebula. Its nebula was discovered serendipitously by Wiersema et al. (2009) and provides the first observational confirmation of jet-driven nebulae from fast-moving LMXBs — a phenomenon that was theoretically predicted (Heinz 2008; Yoon et al. 2011) but had not been observed until recently.

X-ray binary jets are short-lived, radiatively inefficient, and rarely leave extended imprints on their surroundings. SAX J1712.6–3739’s elongated, trail-like nebula preserves the history of jet activity over time, offering a unique into how these jets evolve, dissipate, and interact with the interstellar medium.

The aim of my PhD is to build the first multiwavelength physical model of this system by combining optical polarimetry (FORS2), integral field spectroscopy (MUSE), X-ray data (Chandra, XMM-Newton, Gemini), and radio imaging (ATCA). I aim to characterise the nebular kinematics and morphology, identify dust grain alignment mechanisms, trace spectroscopic signatures of jet–ISM interaction, and constrain the jet’s power, duration, and impact. In addition, X-ray spectroscopy will be used to investigate the nature and accretion state of the compact object powering the system.

I also work on dust extinction mapping techniques in external galaxies, with a focus on resolved stellar photometry.
